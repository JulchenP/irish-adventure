There are quite a few options when it comes to public transportation: The LUAS (tram/light rail system), trains, busses or DublinBikes. In most cases it is quite advantagious to have a Leap Card, which can be topped-up in advance for stress free travelling. You can even use it for the DublinBikes now.
Fun Fact: There's free wifi on all public transport!

Just don't forget to touch-off!

Even though the Irish are known to love their booze (or maybe because of that) there are quite a few things to know about alcohol and the law. The sale of alcoholic drinks is controlled by law, and only licensed businesses can serve or sell alcohol. It is an offence to serve alcohol to a person under 18, and pubs, hotels and restaurants that serve alcohol can do so only during specified hours. Drinking outdoors is not prohibited in general. However, many city and county councils have bye-laws that forbid drinking in some or all public places. I just wouldn't do it ;)

Most shops in Dublin will offer some sort of discount for students:

  • Topshop and Topman: 10% all year round
  • New Look: 10% all year round
  • GAP: 10% all year round
  • OFFICE: 10% all year round
  • VILA: 10% all year round

There are also some food chains that'll grant a discount:

  • MAO: If you fancy swapping out those instant noodles for an upmarket Asian noodle box, then you can use your student card to enjoy an extra 10% off the bill.
  • KC Peaches: If you prefer to eat vegetarian or vegan, you’ll want to take advantage of this student meal offer, where you can get a main meal for just €5.
  • McDonald’s: If you’re doing a bit of shopping and you stumble on a McDonald’s, your student card can get you a €5 extra-value meal on Big Macs, McChicken Sandwiches, and six McNuggets.

Getting around is cheaper too:

  • Bus Éireann: Whether you’re travelling out of Dublin to meet some friends or you just fancy seeing a bit of Ireland, Bus Éireann offers students a 20% discount on already cheap fares, making Irish travel that bit easier.
  • Irish Rail: Although the bus is a nice and cheap way of getting round, the train is significantly faster. Irish Rail offers great discounts on travel for students, with fares between Dublin and Cork costing just €16.19.

You've heard about the DublinBikes by now or maybe you even bought your own bike on gumtree.ie. Cycling in Ireland isn't that much different from cycling in Germany... except for the fact that nobody's looking out for you. So be extra alert! The bike lanes are rather shitty (if they exist at all) and you'll have to cycle on the wrong side of the road (i.e. the left side). Also, get a bike with gears. There aren't that many hills, but better be safe than sorry.

Most shops in Dublin typically open from 9am – 6pm Mondays to Saturdays, extending their opening hours every Thursday until 8pm. Some of the city’s shopping centers and department stores open every day until 8pm or 9pm. Currently, nearly all stores on Sundays are open from midday until 6pm. Some grocery stores are open 24 hours a day, especially the multinational retail chain Spar, which can be found in all of Dublin’s neighborhoods.

Museums in Dublin generally open from 10am to 5pm, Tuesday to Sunday. Most remain open longer hours on Thursday. Most close on Monday.

One of the things I used when moving to Dublin was MeetUp. That's where I found some great friends and also the Dublin German Speakers Group (which I had previously told you about). Apart from that, any pub will do, really. The Irish are a friendly bunch, you won't have a problem with getting to know them.
